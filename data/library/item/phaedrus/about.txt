Gaius Julius Phaedrus was a Roman fabulist from the 1st century AD, known for his adaptations of Aesop's fables into Latin. He was originally a slave from Thrace, later freed by Augustus. He lived during the 1st century AD, under the reigns of Augustus, Tiberius, Caligula, and Claudius. Phaedrus's works are characterized by their concise, witty style and moral lessons. His fables have been influential in Western literature, serving as a key source of animal fables that blend entertainment with ethical teachings. Though not much is known about his life, his contributions to literature have been significant in the preservation and dissemination of fable literature.
