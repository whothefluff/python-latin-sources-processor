Phaedrus's fables are a collection of Latin verse adaptations of Aesop's tales, crafted in the 1st century AD. These fables feature animals endowed with human characteristics, used to convey moral lessons and reflections on human nature and societal norms. Each story is concise and poetic, blending entertainment with ethical teachings. Common themes include the folly of arrogance, the value of wisdom, and the consequences of deceit. Phaedrus's fables stand out for their literary elegance and their ability to engage readers while imparting timeless truths through the simplicity and charm of animal stories.
